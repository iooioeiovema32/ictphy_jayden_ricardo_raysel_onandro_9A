Force and Momentum

Grade 7 Level Physics - Push it to the limit!

What is Force?

Force is an external push or pull that causes an object to accelerate or change its motion. Force is described with Newton’s law. Newton’s first law of motion states that an object at rest will stay at rest unless acted upon by an unbalanced force. Newton’s second law of motion states that the acceleration of an object is dependent on the net force acting upon it and its mass. Newton’s third law states that for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. From Newton’s second law we can construct a formula force that equals mass times acceleration.


Formula: Force = Mass × Acceleration (F = ma)

Force Variety

There are about 8 forces which includes applied force, spring force, drag force, frictional force, normal force, magnetic force, electric force, and gravitational force.

Free Body Diagram

A free body diagram is a simplified illustration that shows an object or a free body isolated from its environment, with all the forces acting on it represented as arrows. The length of each arrow indicates the magnitude of the force, while the direction of the arrow shows the direction in which the force is acting.

Momentum

Momentum is a measure of the motion of an object and is defined as the product of an object’s mass and velocity. Momentum is mass multiplied by velocity. A heavier or faster object has a greater momentum, making it harder to stop or change its motion.

Relationship Between Them

Newton’s second law of motion states that force equals mass times acceleration. Mass times acceleration can be expressed as change in momentum divided by time or rate of change of momentum.

Sound and Waves

Grade 8 Level Physics Sound and Waves

Vibrations & Propagation

Vibrations: Sound begins with a vibration, which is a back and forth motion of particles in an object. Vibration is defined as a periodic motion that occurs when a system is displaced from its equilibrium position and allowed to respond to the force that restores it. This can be proved using a pendulum.


Propagation: Sound travels as a longitudinal wave, meaning the particles of the medium move parallel to the direction of the wave’s travel. This is different from transverse waves, where the motion is perpendicular to the direction of propagation. Wave propagation refers to the movement and transmission of waves. Aside from longitudinal waves and transverse waves, mechanical waves and electromagnetic waves are also included but on different conditions and terms.

Medium

Sound requires a medium to travel through; it cannot propagate in a vacuum. The speed of sound varies depending on the medium, being faster in solids than in liquids and faster in liquids than in gases. Medium is the material that allows energy, waves, or particles to propagate.

Wave Properties

Frequency: Refers to how many times the wave oscillates per second, measured in Hertz (Hz). Frequency determines the pitch of the sound (higher frequency = higher pitch).


Amplitude: The height of the wave, which relates to the loudness or intensity of the sound. Greater amplitude means a louder sound.


Wavelength: Distance between successive crests of a wave, which is inversely related to frequency. Shorter wavelengths correspond to higher frequencies.

Magnetism & Static Electricity

Grade 9 Level Physics - Attractive stuff!

Part 1: Magnetism

Definition

Magnets can attract materials that are made from magnetic materials but cannot attract materials that are made from non-magnetic materials.

Types of magnets

  • Permanent Magnets: Magnets that are made from magnetically hard material, which keeps their magnetism once magnetised.
  • Temporary Magnets: Magnets that are made from magnetically soft material, which lose their magnetism easily.
  • Electromagnets: Magnets that need an electric current to work.

Poles

There are two poles, north and south. If two similar poles are placed near each other, they repel. If two opposite poles are placed near each other, they attract.

Magnetic Fields

Magnetic fields are the volume of space in which we can detect magnetism. It can show the shape, direction, and size of the magnetic field.

Induced Magnetism

Induced magnetism is the temporary magnetization of a material when placed within a magnetic field. It causes magnetic domains to align, turning the material into a temporary magnet that is always attracted to the permanent magnet. Induced magnetism is lost when the material is removed from the field.

Demagnetization

The process of reducing or eliminating the remnant magnetization of a material by disrupting the alignment of its magnetic domains. Some examples include hammering and heating.

Part 2: Static Electricity

Charge Interaction & Law of Electrostatics

Charge Interaction: When an electron meets a proton, they will create an electric force between them and the particles attract each other. When two electrons meet, they repel each other.

Law of Electrostatics: Like charges repel each other. Unlike charges attract each other.

Charge Methods

  • Triboelectric Charging: An object is rubbed with another object, resulting in electrons moving from one object to another.
  • Induction: Charging without making any contact.
  • Conduction: Charging with contact.
  • Discharging: The process of removing the excess charge on an object by means of the transfer of electrons between it and another object of substantial size.

Polarization

The process of separating the charges within an object into two different sides while keeping the object’s overall charge neutral.

Coulomb’s Law

Electrostatic force is the force between two electrical charges. It can be a force of attraction or repulsion.

The magnitude of an electrostatic force depends on the magnitude of each charge (q) and the distance between them (r).

  • Larger the charges, larger the electrostatic force.
  • Larger the distance of the charges, smaller the electrostatic force.

Electric Fields

Electric fields are areas surrounding a charged particle where other charges may experience a force.

  • Opposite charges brought towards each other experience an attractive force.
  • Like charges brought towards each other experience a repulsive force.
